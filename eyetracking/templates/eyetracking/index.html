    Eye Tracking

    Eye tracking involves measuring what a person is looking at and an eye tracker is the device used for making these measurements. The first eye tracker was actually invented to analyse how we read. Since then psychologists have thoroughly studied the reading process. You’ve already read a passage that briefly describes this research, and your eye gaze was recorded as you read.

    An eye tracker is a device that captures where you are looking. It has a lot of applications in human centred computing research and practice. One use is to simply record eye gaze to see where a user has looked, and another is to use eye tracking as a method of input to the computer. The latter use is of special importance when considering disabled people who may not be able to move their hand and therefore not be able to control a mouse or keyboard.

    Why do our eye movements look like this?

    Humans essentially have two type of vision - detailed and peripheral. Detailed vision is when details of a vision scene are actually encoded and is the reason we can do tasks such as reading, driving, watching TV, playing sport, etc. When you look at something the centre of your gaze is where the detailed visual information is encoded. The visual detail drops very rapidly as you move away from the centre of gaze. You can see an example of this in Figure 1.

    Figure 1

    As a result, the eye is always moving so that the centre of gaze can be directed to different parts of the visual stimulus so that visual scene can be put together in the brain. This means we read text in a very specific way, which is illustrated in Figure 2.

    Figure 2

    In more technical terms, the centre of gaze is called a fixation and is where the eye remains (relatively) still to take in visual information. And a saccade is the rapid movement that transports the eye to another fixation. Little to no visual information is encoded during saccades. Figure 2 visually illustrates fixations and saccades; the dots are the fixations and the lines between the fixations are the saccades.

    Figure 3

    So did you fixate on every word? As mentioned above, it has been found that when reading English, people often do not fixate on every word. That means we don’t actually see every single word when we read. Check out Figure 2 again, did you see the second “of”? Most people don’t! You can also see in Figure 3 that not all words are fixated, yet others are fixated more than once. The way in which we read can be related to the difficulty of the text as well as your understanding of the text.

    How is this applicable to Human Centred Computing research?

    Eye trackers were originally used for research such as in psychology to analyse reading behaviour, the way we view images, and to help understand the visual system. These days eye trackers are used a lot in marketing and product design and research into how to make computers work better for humans. An example of this in analysing reading behaviour and adapting eLearning environments based a students’ reading behaviour. This is ongoing research that is being conducted in the HCC group at ANU, the aim of which is to help create learning environments that better help students learn.

    Another way in which eye tracking is used is as an input device for human computer interaction. Some people don’t have the ability to use their hands which restricts their use of computers. They could use their eyes to control a mouse or input keyboard commands!
